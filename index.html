1. Quick Primer — What a Split California King Actually Is

A split California king is a bed system made of two separate mattresses, each 36" wide × 84" long, placed side-by-side on a split adjustable base. Together they create the same 72" × 84" footprint as a regular California king — but each side moves independently. That's the entire point: you can raise your head to read while your partner sleeps flat, and you can each pick a different firmness without compromise.

Don't confuse it with a "split king"

This trips a lot of buyers up. The two are not the same:

SizeTwo-piece dimensionsTotal footprintBest for
Split KingTwo Twin XL: 38" × 80" each76" × 80"Couples under ~6'2" — wider, more standard sheets
Split California KingTwo halves: 36" × 84" each72" × 84"Tall sleepers (6'2"+), narrow rooms, couples who want the extra 4" length
Flex-Top California KingOne mattress, split only at top ~30"72" × 84"Couples who want independent head articulation but a single sleeping surface (no center gap)
Heads up Split California king is a niche size. Only a handful of brands make true split Cal king mattresses — Saatva and Tempur-Pedic are the two heavyweights. Bedding selection is also more limited than standard sizes, so factor sheets into your decision before you commit.

3. How to Attach a Headboard to a Split Cal King Setup

There are three viable approaches: bolt-on brackets to the adjustable bases, a freestanding "captured" headboard inside an existing bed frame, or a wall-mounted floating headboard. Each has trade-offs.

Option A — Bolt-on brackets (most common)

Most modern adjustable bases (Saatva, Tempur-Pedic, Nectar, GhostBed) ship with — or have an add-on kit for — universal headboard brackets. Because a split Cal king has two bases, you'll need two bracket kits (one per base) and a headboard wide enough to span both.

  1. Confirm your bases have headboard mounting holes or pre-installed brackets. Look on the side rails near the head — most have either a "T" bracket or pre-drilled mounting holes. If not, order a universal headboard bracket kit (~$30–$60).
  2. Buy a king-sized headboard, not Cal king. A standard king headboard (78"–80" wide) covers the 72" combined width of two split Cal king bases with a bit of overhang on each side, which looks better than a too-narrow Cal king headboard.
  3. Raise the head of both bases to make working underneath easier, and clear the area.
  4. Bolt the bracket channels to each base frame. Tighten with a ½" wrench or socket. Repeat on the second base.
  5. Attach the bracket flange to the headboard (or slide hook-on style). Keep the headboard 1½" to 2" away from the mattress edge — this clearance is critical so the mattress doesn't rub the headboard when the base articulates.
  6. Test articulate the bases up and down a few times. The mattress should slide cleanly past the headboard with no catching.

Option B — Captured inside an existing bed frame

If you already have a king or California king bed frame with a headboard, footboard, and side rails, you can just set the two adjustable bases inside it. No bracket installation needed. The frame's interior dimensions need to match the king or Cal king size of your bases. Make sure to use a zero-clearance adjustable base if the frame doesn't have much vertical room.

Option C — Wall-mounted (floating) headboard

The cleanest modern look. The headboard mounts to the wall studs behind the bed, completely independent of the bases. This means it doesn't move when the bases articulate, which some people prefer. Use a stud finder, a level, and heavy-duty wall anchors. Center it on the 72" combined width of the two bases.

Pro tip for split bases Many couples skip the brackets entirely and use a freestanding upholstered headboard pinned between the bed and the wall. This works great with split bases because there's nothing to align — the bed simply pushes the headboard against the wall.

4. What Kind of Mattress You Need

Not all mattresses bend. Split Cal king is built around a dual adjustable base, so the mattress has to articulate without damaging itself. Here's what to look for and what to avoid.

✓ Mattress types that work

  • All-foam (memory foam, polyfoam) — flexes easily, no internal damage
  • Latex — naturally flexible, very durable on adjustable bases
  • Foam-or-latex hybrids — coils designed to flex with adjustable bases (most modern hybrids qualify)
  • Air-chamber (e.g., Saatva Solaire, Sleep Number) — specifically engineered for adjustable use

✗ Mattress types to avoid

  • Traditional innerspring with rigid coils — bending voids the warranty on many models
  • Mattresses over 13–14" thick — get harder to bend and trap heat when articulated
  • Pillow-top "no-flip" beds not explicitly rated for adjustable bases

Firmness & sleep position guidance

6. Fitted Sheets & Bedding — What Actually Works

Sheets are the trickiest part of owning a split Cal king. Standard Cal king sheets do not fit. You have a few options, and which you pick depends on whether your two halves are independent (true split) or share a foot section (flex-top).

The three sheet styles to know

StyleHow it fitsBest for
Two Twin XL-Long fitted sheets (each 36" × 84")One per mattress half, no center connectionTrue split Cal king (the Saatva / Tempur-Pedic split Cal king setup) — easiest to wash; eliminates sheet-stealing
Flex-top / Split-head fitted sheetOne unified bottom, split only at the top ~30"Sleep Number FlexTop California King and similar single-mattress flex-top beds
Single Cal king fitted sheet (over both halves)One sheet stretched over both mattressesCouples who prefer the look of one bed but lose some independent articulation; sheet binds when bases tilt apart

How to actually put them on

  1. Identify your style. If you have two physically separate mattresses, you need two Twin XL-Long fitted sheets (note: regular Twin XL is 80" — for split Cal king you need 84" length). If you have a flex-top, get a flex-top fitted sheet sized for Cal king.
  2. Look for deep pockets — 16" minimum. Modern mattresses with toppers are often 14"–18" thick. Sheets with shallow 12" pockets pop off when the base articulates.
  3. Anchor with sheet straps or a "cinch-fit" elastic system. This is the single biggest tip for adjustable beds. Brands like CinchFit and QuickZip make sheets with continuous elastic that survives nightly head-raising without popping off.
  4. Use split top sheets. Two separate top sheets — one per side — let each of you pick a different weight and prevent the cover-stealing battle. Common in Scandinavian-style bedding.
  5. Skip the comforter, use two duvets. The Scandinavian method: each person has their own duvet. Permanently solves cover-stealing and works perfectly with a split base.
